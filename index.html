CodeX is a global community of healthcare stakeholders currently working on data standards in three domains. We build and implement in the field and on-site.

Cancer

The first domain that the CodeX community tackled is cancer. Using the minimal Common Oncology Data Elements (mCODE), an open data standard enabling enabled high-quality data capture for cancer that can be widely adopted.

70+ oncology definitions in use at Dana-Farber Cancer Institute, MD Anderson Center, Massachusetts General Hospital and 44 other institutions. Versions of the mCODE and associated modeling tools are currently operational in over 250 organizations.

Our History

Innovation can sometimes strike at unexpected moments. A chance encounter, Dr. Jay Schnitzer sat on a plane besides a former colleague. He and Dr. Monica Bertagnolli had trained together as surgeons once upon a time at Brigham and Women’s Hospital and had remained friends. They started catching up on their current endeavors. Dr. Bertagnolli, a cancer surgeon at Brigham and Women’s and Dana Farber, was also leading the Alliance for Clinical Trials in Oncology. She was passionate about finding ways to transform the way clinical trials are conducted, for example, by using electronic health records (EHR) to increase diverse data and broaden the range of trial participants. Dr. Schnitzer, as the Chief Technology Officer at MITRE, was leading a wide-ranging research program, including a focus on healthcare. He spoke about MITRE’s quest to drive data standards for EHR systems so that patient data could be aggregated and searched for better treatments and new therapies.

The two got out a napkin and started jotting down ideas for a standard health record. This led to the partnership that created mCODE, minimal Common Oncology Data Elements. Today, what began as an impromptu meeting of old friends and scribbled ideas on an airplane napkin is a robust community creating clinical specialty standards to transform cancer patient care and research. Someday, Drs Bertagnolli and Schnitzer and their many partners hope to use data standards to achieve a Learning Health System, one in which each interaction between a clinician and patient provides high-quality data leading to safer care, improved outcomes, and lower costs for everyone.
